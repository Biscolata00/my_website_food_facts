What is a watermelon?

Watermelons, or citrullus lanatus, are cucurbitaceae which we consume as a fruit. They usually have a green hard peel, either striped or more uniformly dark green, and a red flesh although alternative colors can be found (e.g. orange). The shape varies from round to oval [1–4]. It should be noted that watermelons that have been grown in molds to achieve specific forms, e.g. square in Japan, are usually meant as decoration and not as fruits destined for consumption [5,6].

The red flesh, which is the endocarp of the fruit [4], is the part most commonly eaten, but the rind (the white flesh between the red flesh and the tough green peel) and the seeds can also be consumed.

Their season for watermelon is from May to August [1,7].

How to recognize a good one

Recognizing a good watermelon may be challenging; however, there is a collection of indicators to look for. The more hints are inspected, the better the chances to pick a ripe, undamaged watermelon [2,7,8].

Heaviness: a heavy watermelon indicates a high content in water, i.e. a juicier fruit. Heaviness must be checked relatively between watermelons of the same size.

Yellow stain: a yellow stain indicates where the watermelon was in contact with the earth. Usually, the yellower the stain, the better, as it indicates that the fruit ripened in the sun. However, the final color might also be influenced by the watermelon variety, with some varieties’ spots turning less yellow than others.

Reverberating, hollow sound: when lightly thumping a watermelon, the sound should be uniform and hollow, similar to a basketball. This indicates that the inside of the fruit was not damaged; on the other hand, a flat or ununiform sound suggests that the fruit sustained a shock, leading water to leak from the cells and to absorb the sound.

Shriveled stem: when a watermelon is ripe, it starts detaching itself from the vine and the stem shrivels and dries. A shriveled steam thus indicated a ripe watermelon, while a torn stem may indicate a forceful removal of an unripe fruit.

Contrasting stripes: for watermelon with stripes, one should look for blocky stripes with a high contrast. Low contrast stripes may indicate under-ripeness, while mixed colors within single stripes hint at over-ripeness (e.g. yellow in the dark lines).

If the flesh is visible, e.g. when purchasing precut fruits, two other signs may be accounted for:

Red flesh: the redder the flesh (or darker colored in case of other hues), the ripper and sweeter the fruit.

Sugar crystals: a high amount of sugar leads to small crystals forming around the seeds.

Contrary to popular belief, peel scarring, also referred to as webbing, is not a sign of sweetness. Scarring is commonly thought to be a sign of bee pollination, which is known to lead to a sweeter fruit [9]; however, webbing is actually caused by mechanical damages, usually during the early stages of the fruit development [10]. This does not however mean that the final fruit is damaged or of lesser quality.

If a watermelon presents soft peel, foul smell, odd taste or traces of mold [11], dispose of it. To preventively delay a watermelon from going bad, store it in the fridge, especially once open. Watermelon can also be frozen although this will lead to a change in texture, meaning it will not be pleasant to it as such once thawed (it can be turned into drinks or dishes that call for a different texture). After being removed from the vine, watermelons keep for 2-3 weeks for 10-15°C at 90% humidity [4].

Consuming watermelon

Watermelon is usually consumed fresh on its own. In its fresh taste, it can also be sprinkled with salt which acts as a sweetness and taste enhancer [12]. Cubed fresh watermelon can be integrated in salads [2].

Watermelon can be also be grinded down into drinks, such as watermelon lemonades, smoothies or juices, or even soups (e.g. watermelon gazpacho)

Watermelon steaks have also gained in popularity; a thick slick of watermelon can be brushed on with an umami, savory marinade for 10 minutes to a few hours before being pan grilled or barbecued. The goal is to cook the fruit completely, not only provoke the maillard reaction [2]. Similarly, watermelon can also be baked in the oven. In this cooked form, it has been put forward by vegetarians/vegans as a tuna alternative due to similarities in texture and the capacity of watermelon to be infused with umami flavors [13].

A few ingredients that are known to pair especially well with watermelon include feta, goat cheese, lemon, mint, pepper, salt, tomatoes and vinegar [13].

What to do with an underripe watermelon

If the chosen watermelon still ends up being underripe, i.e. lacking sweetness and taste, it can be improved by infusing it with a syrup by letting watermelon cubes infuse in syrup in a sous-vide environment. This can be the occasion to bring in other flavors by making a specific syrup, e.g. a vanilla syrup [14]. After 30 minutes of marinating, the fruit should be regularly checked lest its texture suffers. The result can be consumed as the fresh fruit would be.

Otherwise, the fruit can be integrated into a dish or a drink where sugar or seasoning will have the role of bringing in flavor.

What to do with an overripe watermelon

On the contrary, if a watermelon is overripe, which usually translates into an unpleasantly soft and mealy texture, it may be best to turn it into a drink, a soup or a sorbet [15], the goal being to enjoy the taste while discarding the unpleasant texture.

Consuming the rind

Watermelon rind, which is the white part between the green skin and the red flesh, can also be consumed. This part is less sweet than the red flesh and crunchier [2]. It can be candied [16], pickled [17], or used in stir fries [18]. The rind can also be added to blended watermelon drinks to increase the fiber intake and make the drink more filling [19]. The rind being less sweet than the flesh and less refreshing, it should represent 10-20% maximum of the drink to not diminish the flavor too much [20]. Consuming the rind is also a good way to reduce food waste at an individual scale.

Some experiments are also ongoing to determine the possibility of using watermelon rind at an industrial scale, for instance with dried rind powder which can be used to enrich wheat flour (1% concentration) to achieve softer, moister and higher rising bread thanks to the rind fiber content (55g fiber/100g dried powder) [21].

Consuming the seeds

Even the seeds can be consumed. While some options are not entirely accessible for a home cook, such as grinding the seeds into a flour [22], it is also possible to roast the seeds to turn them into a snack [23], as a topping for salads [2], or as a sweet treat, e.g. the Indian chikki[24] .

Recent research has been exploring the properties of watermelon seed flour, which has a high protein content (82g/100g [22] when defatted, 54.48 g/100 g protein if not [25]). While watermelon seeds flour is not readily commercially available, research has shown that it can be used to improve some baked good such as bread at 2.5% for 97.5% wheat flour [26] or cookies for which all crispiness, coloration and nutrition were improved (although with a slightly affected texture and flavor scores) [27].

Health

Watermelons being made of 91-93% water, they represent a good source of hydration [1,3,4] and can contribute to reaching the recommended daily fluid intake [28].

The flesh of the watermelon is low in sugars and fat while constituting a great source of vitamin A (25% daily needs/100g, which represents around one slice) and vitamin B9 (18% daily needs/100g). Additionally, 100g cover 5% of one's daily needs for vitamin C [1].

As previously mentioned, seeds and rind consumed in a dehydrated form become an interesting source of fiber and protein (55g fiber/100g dried watermelon rind powder [21] and up to 82g/100g of protein for flour made from the seeds [22]).

Discussions around GMOs and seedless

While alarming discourses can be heard about seedless watermelons and GMOs, it is important to note that 1) GMOs can be beneficial and harmless 2) seedless watermelons are not GMOs. Instead, they are a hybrid obtained by breeding a male watermelon from a variety with 4 sets of chromosomes (a tetraploid) with a female watermelon of a variety with only two sets of chromosomes (a diploid). The resulting hybrid has thus three sets of chromosomes (a triploid), which can’t divide equally, leading to a sterile fruit with no viable pollen and no or only few underdeveloped seeds [4,29]. This technique is rather recent since it has been developed from 1939 onward. It requires the tetraploid variety to take a third of the field to ensure efficient cross-breeding [4].

Manipulations of the sort to gain control over the seeds is not limited to seedless watermelon, as dark seeds may be desirable for aesthetic purposes, or large seeds for confectionary purposes. Hybrids generally represent a large part of the commercialized watermelons [4].

Farming, ethics and sustainability

Watermelons have been farmed in Africa for over four thousand years [4]. In the last centuries, watermelon cultivation has spread all over the world. The biggest producer is China by far, wielding 64 millions tons in 2023, followed by India (3.6 M tons) and Turkey (3.1 M tons) [30]. Producing this fruit comes with its share of challenges; first, there are non-negligible risks of drought as the plant requires an above average amount of water. The plant itself has deep roots to be able to reach water, but farmers also look for solutions such as pumping groundwater, to the extent of emptying natural resources, or even turning towards desalinization which is a costly strategy leading to a high energy consumption [2,29]. Those issues are also enhanced by the expanding demand for watermelon which leads producers to deliver fruits from February to October instead of the original May-August timeframe [29]. Therefore, consuming watermelon in season is a possible individual action to avoid enhancing this issue.

As it is often the case in farming industries, poor working conditions or even abuse have been reported. Issues range from poor housing and working conditions and low wages to withholding of wages and/or passports. Such problems have been reported across the globe, e.g. in the USA [31,32] or in Spain [33,34].
