Basic facts
Watermelons are mostly composed of water (91-93%)
There are variations in the shapes and colors
May to August is their natural season
Selecting a good watermelon
Heaviness is a sign of juiciness
A yellow stain on the peel indicates the fruit ripened under the sun
A hollow sound indicates a lack of internal damage
A shriveled stem indicated the watermelon ripened on the plant long enough, while a torn stem warns of the contrary
Contrasting, clearly defined stripes hints at ripeness, while low contrast indicated underripeness and yellowing stripes suggest overripeness
Red flesh is a sign of sweetness
Crystal sugar around the seeds form if the fruit is sweet enough
Peel scarring/webbing is not and indicator of ripeness
Consumption
Fresh: as such, with salt, in salads, infused with syrup (later fitting for overripe fruits)
Liquid form: smoothies, juices, lemonades, soups, sorbets (all fitting for over and underripe fruits as well)
Cooked: grilled, baked
Rind: as an additive to watermelon flesh smoothies, pickled, candied, in stir fries
Seeds: roasted
Health
Watermelons being 91-93% water makes them a good source of hydration
A 100g portion of watermelon is enough to cover a fourth of the daily needs for vitamin A, 18% of the needs of vitamin B9 as well as a small amount of vitamin C
Dehydrated powders made from rind or seeds have a high amount of fibers
Seedless watermelons are not detrimental to health are they are simply the results of always renewed hybridization
Farming, Ethics and Sustainability
Watermelons has been cultivated first in Africa
China is now leading watermelon production, followed by India and Turkey
Watermelons require sunny and hot climates yet an important amount of water to grow well, sometimes making their cultivation a challenge when it comes to sustainably sourcing water
Increasing demand throughout the year makes cultivating sustainably all the harder
There have been many cases of reported worker abuse in western countries
