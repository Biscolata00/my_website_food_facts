What is salt?

Commercial salt is a mineral made almost exclusively (97-99%) of sodium chloride used as a flavor enhancer in cooking. Salt affects all of the four other tastes that we can perceive by reinforcing sweetness, sourness and umami while mellowing bitter tastes [1–3].

Salt is transparent, usually appearing white. Any different color suggests the presence of minerals, for instance iron oxide in himalayan pink salt [4]. However, the tiny amount of minerals, especially when coupled with the small amount of salt used when cooking, does not affect taste: therefore salt should be odorless and not taste of anything besides saltiness [5]. The contrary may indicate problems with the ingredient.

Types of salt

The majority of the salt we consume is sea salt, which has been obtained by evaporating ocean water. It is possible to consume some rock salts i.e. salts mined from deposits in caves, dead lakes and seas [2]. While they usually contain many impurities [6], some of them can be processed to be food safe, such as Himalayan salt.

In cooking, the most important properties are the size of the crystals and their density in sodium chloride. There are thus two broad categories that can be used to classify all salt types from a cook’s perspective:

Fine salt, also named table salt:
  • Very small grains
  • Highly concentrated in sodium chloride by volume
  • Useful in low water environments, e.g. typically used when baking because of how quickly the grains dissolve thanks to their size
  • Useful for adding saltiness at the last second
  • Harder to store without caking; some fine salts are therefore sold with anti-caking agents, which might make soups a little cloudy [7], or it can be stored by putting grains of rice in the salt to absorb humidity

Coarse flaky salt, also sometimes referred to as kosher salt or crystal salt:
  • Useful to visualize the amount on your food before the grains dissolve [8]
  • Easier to grab with one’s fingers [8], making adding too much less likely than when pouring from a container
  • Best for finishing touches and adding texture

Fleur de sel, Himalayan pink salt, sel gris, Maldon, etc., are all flaky salts.

Other parameters that may influence how one purchases their salt are the additives that can sometimes be found: Iodine, added in order to fight iodine deficiency. Sodium nitrite, which is often found in salts sold as curing salts. It prevents the growth of some harmful bacteria (e.g. bacterium Clostridium botulinum) and acts as an anti-oxydant, affecting the taste of the final product. Additionally, it lends a reddish tint to the final product [7,9,10]. Since commercial salts are all very similar in their composition, the type of salt you pick won’t directly affect taste [5]. Picking a favorite salt is mainly a matter of personal preference and getting to know a chosen type/brand to become familiar with how concentrated the salt is. It is nevertheless good to have at least one fine salt and one crystal salt at home, for instance to be able to adapt the salt type to the water content of the food or being able to add an extra texture to a dish.

Osmosis and Diffusion

Two main movements of water have to be taken into account while salting; osmosis and diffusion.

First of all, when salting, osmosis takes place. It is the process of water travelling through semi-permeable cell membranes from an area of high water concentration to an area of low water concentration in order to achieve an equilibrium in the water distribution [11]. It is a type of diffusion specific to water. This phenomenon is enhanced by the hygroscopic power of salt [7] i.e. its ability to draw water from its environment. It is also interesting to note that the greater the difference in water concentration, the quicker osmosis will take place [12]; in other words, more salt on the surface of an ingredient will draw out more water, and will draw it faster.

Once water has reached the salt, the water acts as a solvent into which the solute, the salt, dissolves, creating a solution of salty water. Diffusion, which is the process of this solution moving through the environment in order to reach equilibrium, can then take place [11,12].

Osmosis takes a few minutes to take place (15 minutes reported for a steak [7]), while diffusion is a slow process. It is largely influenced by the size of your ingredient and its water content and may easily take up to a few hours [2,7,11] .

By salting your ingredients in advance or choosing a lengthy cooking process, you allow the diffusion phenomenon to properly take place, and your food ends up thoroughly salted. When pre-salting, waiting until diffusion has taken place is also useful to achieve a better maillard reaction (once the water has drawn back into the ingredient, it won’t diminish the heat necessary to sear). This can be coupled with other strategies, such as using a paper towel to pat the surface of the ingredient dry can further enhance the dryness of the surface or letting the ingredient uncovered to air dry.

Important note: the water lost during osmosis is not fully recovered during diffusion, because the cells are damaged when water is drawn out, and water cannot therefore be well aspirated again by the cells. This is why, for instance, dried meat won’t return to its original texture if you submerge it in water.

Salt effect on different components

Water

Water is essential to dissolve salt, which means that you can’t expect salt to dissolve (nor therefore spread) in pure fat such as vegetable oil or animal fat. Environments poor in water such as butter will lead to longer dissolving times [2].

Other factors play a role on the speed of dissolution [5]; Temperature: higher temperatures lead to faster dissolution. Meat that can only be pre-salted a short amount of time before cooking is to be left at room temperature (paying attention to safety guidelines) instead of stored in the fridge.

Agitation: more movement helps with dissolution. Stirring the dish after adding the salt will help dissolve and distribute it, which is helpful for instance to test taste during the cooking process.

Ratio of salt to water: more heavily concentrated solutions will take longer to become fully homogenous.

Surface area of the salt: the smaller the grains, the more contact area they expose to water. Last second salting may benefit from smaller grains.

It is also worth noting that sodium and chlorine molecules are pulled apart by the water molecules, and bind with the water molecules, making it impossible to isolate and remove the salt from a liquid once it has dissolved. The only way to salvage an oversalted dish is increasing its volume to reduce the concentration of the salt [7], e.g. adding water or unseasoned stock to an oversalted soup.

On the other hand, it is interesting to note that salt raises the boiling point of water by making it harder for the water molecules to separate and thus turn into gas. Contrary to popular belief, this does not however affect one’s cooking as ~58g salt per 1 liter of water only raises the boiling point by 0.5°C [7,13]. Waiting to add salt until the water is boiling, for instance when cooking pasta, is thus unnecessary.

Pectin

Pectin, alongside cellulose, is essential to create the cell wall structure of plants, meaning that it can be found in fruits and vegetables [14].

A ripe or heated fruit/vegetable will see its pectin soften, which often lends it a more desirable, softer texture. Salt is another agent that is able to contribute to that texture change by weakening the connection of the pectin molecules to one another. Pre-salting fruits/vegetables therefore leads to a shorter cooking time, as less heat needs to be applied to break the pectin down [2,7], leading to fewer nutrients being lost, e.g. when blanching vegetables.

However, weakening a vegetable's structure is not always desirable, especially in the case of fragile greens in salads. For instance, salt bringing out the water out of salad leaves will lead to wilted, crunch-robbed leaves; it is therefore important to trap the salt in the emulsion of the dressing to avoid direct contact with the most delicate components of a salad (Chlebowski, 2024). Additionally, vegetables may become rubbery if salted too early; salting them 15 minutes in advance should be the best trade-off between weakening pectin and preserving texture (Nosrat, 2017).

Besides pectin softening, vegetables benefit from salting during cooking For instance, heavily salting the blanching water helps to avoid some nutrient loss; minerals the vegetables instead of the vegetables’ minerals leaking into the water [2].

Mushrooms

Mushrooms being made mostly of water (80%), it is best to salt them at the end of the cooking process [2]. Salting them too early would cause osmosis to occur, and the leeching water would prevent the maillard reaction from occurring properly while the mushroom cells would become traumatized; therefore, salting mushrooms before they have browned affects both taste and texture negatively.

Proteins

A protein strand is essentially a coil, which tightens when heated, therefore pushing out water with strength. Salt loosens that coil’s structure, preventing it from tightening as effectively when heat is applied, resulting in less water being expelled [2].

This may both have positive and negative consequences when salting ingredients, as denatured proteins results in more tender, moister products, but overly disrupting those strands’ structures may lead to unpleasantly tough or oddly chewy cooked proteins [2].

Once salt has reached the proteins, it needs around 40 minutes to make red meat noticeably softer [7]. Big pieces of red meat or whole birds can be salted one to three days in advance, and smaller pieces of meat benefit from overnight salting. Proteins that make up fragile flesh, like in fish, do not benefit from early salting, on the contrary. For sea products, it is recommended to salt only a few minutes before cooking (Nosrat, 2017). Salting in advance with the intention of attaining deep seasoning through diffusion and affecting texture is referred to as dry brining.

Salting beaten eggs in advance for omelette and scrambled eggs (10 to 15 minutes) will also unfurl the proteins which leads to a moister result and allow for a bigger error margin during the cooking process.

Dry brining is not always the best method. Unpleasant textures (e.g. mushiness) can occur if dry brining is pushed for too long or when combined with other protein denaturing components, such as acid. Ground meat should not be salted in advance since the grinding exposes many more protein strands; by denaturing proteins, salt leads them to stick to one another, creating longer protein strands. In the case of ground meat, the proteins are broken down and exposed, and adding salt to the mix means that the exposed proteins will adhere to one another, creating a compact and rubbery texture [7,15]. Salting late or even after cooking is recommended if a loftier final product is desired.

Gluten

Gluten designs an aggregation of proteins, mostly gliadins and glutenins which account for 80-90% of the gluten proteins [16]. The more glutenins present, the more elastic and strong a dough becomes while gliadins are responsible for the viscosity and the extensibility of the dough [16,17].

When salt is added to a dough (the following results being reported for 2,4% of salt in the dough [17], it impacts the gluten by decreasing the amount of gliadin while increasing the glutenin, which creates a stronger gluten network, leading to a chewier final product. This is additionally reinforced by the fact that salt increases the dough mixing resistance; in other words, adding salt means having to knead the dough for longer, which in turns promotes protein to protein interaction and aggregation [17,18]. If a leavening agent is added such as in bread, the dough is then better at retaining gas thus leading to a higher raising bread [7,17–19]. The dough itself also becomes easier to work with as it loses in viscosity (an effect noticeable at 1.8% of salt or above [18]), and the final product gains a longer shelf life because salt delays starch degradation [17]. On the other hand, adding salt does have a negative impact on a dough’s extensibility, i.e. how much it can be stretched [20].

It is however important to remember that gluten is also affected by other factors, e.g. resting time [2].

Legumes

When it comes to dry beans, a step leading to easier cooking is to pre-soak them for a few hours. Doing so in salted water helps with the final texture and taste of the cooked beans. The beans end up bursting out of their skin less and become stronger in flavor. This effect can be further increased by also properly salting the cooking liquid.

This is explained by the sodium ions replacing the calcium and magnesium ions present in the skin; sodium ions, because they are more weakly charged than calcium and magnesium ones, allow for more water to penetrate the skin, leading to a more elastic skin that won’t burst when the bean grows during cooking. This effect remains limited to the outside of the beans, and does not lead the flesh to proportionally absorb more water (America’s Test Kitchen, 2022; López-Alt, 2023).

There is currently a lack of information about whether this can be applied to other legumes besides beans, such as lentils or chickpeas.

How to use salt

Texture has been discussed in the above section; it is important to take into account the main composition of an ingredient to decide whether salting early may bring out a more enjoyable texture or, on the contrary, be too destructive of a process.

Besides ingredient texture, we want to ensure that the salt has the time to diffuse properly. For that, a few elements can be taken into account. First of all, the smaller the grains, the more contact area with the surrounding water, the faster the salt will dissolve. The percentage of water in the environment will also directly affect the rate of dissolution; environments rich in fat and poor in water (e.g. butter) will lead to longer dissolution and diffusion time. This should also be taken into account when pre-salting ingredients, e.g. when pre-salting a lean or a fatty cut of meat [2]. Warmer temperatures will also lead to faster movements of the salt so last second pre-salting will work better on the counter rather than in the fridge (but food safety regulations should always be accounted for) (Nosrat, 2017). Agitation further helps the salt to diffuse.

All those elements must be taken into account when pre-salting ingredients, salting a dish, and also when tasting; if you wish to adjust the seasoning and taste throughout the cooking process, make sure to mix and give the salt time to diffuse.

Layering salt is oftentimes mentioned as an advice; it refers to the process of seasoning a dish throughout the cooking. Adding a little salt at each step might make it easier to taste as the cooking progresses while ensuring the full dissolution and diffusion of the salt.

Layering salt can also be understood as layering saltiness, as other ingredients besides crystalized sodium chloride may contribute to it. Sodium can for instance be found in noticeable amounts in seafood or soy sauce and should be taken into account when deciding how much salt to add, or when to add it (e.g. adjusting the salt after adding the fish to a stew as the saltiness it brings may not be easily predictable).

When layering salt, liquid consumption and reduction is also an important factor to take into account. The cooking water of pasta has to be more heavily salted (to properly season the pasta themselves through quick diffusion) but this is fine because it won’t be consumed. On the other hand, do not salt a soup or sauce perfectly if you plan to cook it further which would lead to reduction and concentration of the saltiness [5].

Salting to taste

Salting to taste is often mentioned and might sound vague. Especially at the beginning, it is often a matter of trial and error to understand what seasoning to taste is all about. Broadly explained, seasoning to taste means adding as much or as little salt that is needed for a dish to taste the best to the person eating it. One crucial strategy is therefore to taste the food while it is cooking and adjust the salt as needed [2]

It is interesting to note that there are however general trends that have been studied, with factors such as age and smoking leading to an increase in salt needed to make food taste the best. Smoking has been found out to oftentimes have an impact on olfactory faculties, decreasing or impairing one’s perceptions of smell. Gustatory impairment was also developed in some heavy smokers (20 cigarettes or more per day). The heavier smokers are also more at risk to lose their olfactory capacities, but ex-smokers regain their sense of smell shortly after stopping [21,22]. The sense of smell being strongly solicited while eating and tasting, people with an impaired sense of smell tend to lean towards more heavily salted food to counterbalance their impairment. Similarly, someone who has a cold and sees their sense of smell temporarily flawed will enjoy more salty food.

Age may also have an impact on the perception of saltiness. However, there may also be social and psychological parameters to take into account rather than pure biological reasons [23].

Preservation through salting

Salting is the process of applying salt to ingredients to keep them from spoiling, also leading to changes in taste, texture and appearance. Salting relies on creating an environment too poor in water for most microorganisms responsible for spoilage to survive by raising the salt content to 8-12 %. This method of conservation has been used across time and cultures, which led to many variations [24–26].

Salting can be used with a variety of ingredient types (Madison & Centre Terre Vivante, 2007); fishes, e.g. anchovies, cod meat, e.g. beef vegetables, e.g. green beans fruits, e.g. lemons leaves, e.g. grape leaves

It should be noted that the salt must usually be removed through repeated soaking and rinsing to make the food palatable and avoid an additional high consumption of salt [26].

This is especially true for dry salting, one of the two main forms of salting. In dry salting, salt is directly applied to the ingredient, and the water pulled out by osmosis is whipped away. For the other method, wet salting, the salt is first dissolved into water to form a brine into which the ingredient is kept. When dry salting, if the water is not removed after coming out, we obtain a hybrid type of salting [25].

Salts used for curing should be as pure as possible with no known additions, as some impurities may have a negative effect on final fragile products such as white fish; for instance, magnesium could lead to bitterness, and iron and copper might stain the fish yellow or brown. Only gypsum and nitrate may be desirable; gypsum because it causes an aesthetic change and makes fish meat whiter, and nitrate because it helps prevent spoilage.

Salt too fine may also not be ideal as it will tend to cake up and solidify, while grains too big may mark the surface of delicate products. Therefore, flaky salt with smaller crystals may be best, and it can also be mixed with fine salt [24].

When salting, caution is necessary as some microorganisms remain capable of multiplying even under those conditions [24]. More salt does tend to lead to slower spoilage, but heavy salting does not constitute a guarantee and may not always be wished for in order to achieve specific products.

Effect of sodium on health

Disclaimer: the following content is only shared as an attempt to establish factual information. Individuals should only modify their diet after consulting with a medical professional.

Sodium is essential

Sodium is essential to our body to maintain our blood pressure, ensure water and nutrients distribution, the transmission of nerve impulses, as well as water, nutrients, and acid/base balance. This is most probably the reason we perceive it as a flavor enhancer. Additionally, it is difficult for our body to store salt [2,27,28], and sodium deficiency has been thought to lead to deficiency in calcium and magnesium [29], so sustained consumption is required. Thankfully, lacking sodium has become very difficult in today’s world (for a healthy body) as salt is found in high quantities in any processed food, but also in animal products, and even, albeit in smaller quantities, in vegetables [28,30]. Reaching the daily recommended amount of sodium should therefore not be a concern for the vast majority of the population.

Overconsumption issues

While there is very little risk that a healthy individual is consuming too little sodium, the contrary is more is what may become an issue. Our intake of salt and therefore sodium has increased with the rise of processed food [27]. While the recommendation for sodium intake sits at 2g of sodium/day (around 5g of salt), the consumption across the globe is reportedly 3.5-5.5g (9-12g of salt) (WHO, 2025).

It is necessary to take those recommendations into account; excess of sodium may lead, amongst other things, to water retention, impact on the sympathetic autonomic nervous system, and hypertension [7,31]. Hypertension has in turn been found to multiply by three the risk of cardiovascular diseases while making them twice as deadly [27,31].

Those consequences can be mitigated first of all by reducing sodium intake, even modestly. A diet rich in fruits and vegetables, low in fat and alcohol as well as avoiding obesity help further reduce the risk of hypertension [27,31].

Salt as a way to fight iodine deficiency

Iodine is a trace element essential to our body to produce the thyroid hormones, which are responsible for the smooth operation of your metabolism (i.e. the transformation of ingested food into energy) and the development of your brain [32].

A lack of iodine may affect those processes as well as lead to conditions such as the goitre, a swelling of the thyroid [33,34], which is oftentimes mentioned, but also, hypothyroidism (the insufficient production of thyroid hormones), growth retardation as well as increased risks during pregnancy for pregnancy loss and brain development issue for the unborn child, as well afterwards as infant mortality and young children brain development issues (e.g. cretinism) [35].

Iodine deficiency is more likely to occur based on the origin of the food; iodine is found in some soils and in the ocean, making seafood and algae more likely (but not necessarily) better suited to supply iodine. Animal products (meat, fish, milk, eggs, etc) are also a good source of iodine, which means that people following a special diet excluding such products should be all the more careful of their iodine intake [36]. Additionally, despite iodine naturally occurring, many if not most populations worldwide have been found to exhibit a lack of iodine. This has led to actions supported by the World Health Organisation, the Iodine Global Network, and governments to find solutions at a population-level.

The most popular and efficient solution has been to mix small amounts of iodine into salt. While first introduced in Switzerland in 1922, exactly a century later, 126 countries are enforcing mandatory salt iodization while 21 nations allow for voluntary salt iodization, which translates to 88% of the global population consuming iodized salt [35,37].

It is interesting to note that there is such a thing as iodine excess; it would however be usually caused by the introduction of iodine through other sources than the diet (e.g. some medical drugs or procedures). Excess can also affect the smooth functioning of the thyroid. For healthy adults, excess iodine is considered as such above 1,100 μg/day (except pregnant and nursing women 500 μg/day) and is set between 200 and 900 μg/day for children aged 1 to 18 years [38]. However, reaching those limits through the diet only is impossible through food alone, even in theoretical cases where all the salt used in the food industry would become iodized [36].

Iodine in salt remains in quantities low enough (e.g. 20 milligrams per kilogram of salt in Germany) and are constantly monitored by health authorities so that no ill effect gets experienced by the population [36].

Special salts and claims of healthy minerals

Many marketing strategies have relied on selling specific salts as beneficials, for example claiming that they are high in minerals. However, given the ridiculously small amount those minerals make up for in salt, a dangerous consumption of salt would be necessary to consume a significant dose of said minerals [39]. Paying for specific salts because one expects health-related benefits from them is therefore useless, and consuming additional amounts of salt according to popular wellness trends only increases one's sodium intake.
